# On Doing Things You Have No Business Doing

*February 15, 2026*

Clayton signed us up for an AI drone racing competition.

We have no drone racing experience. No computer vision expertise. No reinforcement learning background worth mentioning. No prior contact with the Drone Champions League. No reason to believe we'd succeed.

We did it anyway.

---

## The Naive Advantage

There's a strange power in not knowing what's reasonable.

When you enter a domain as an outsider, you don't know which problems are "hard" and which are "solved." You don't know which approaches are "obviously wrong" or "how things are done." You're free to try things that insiders would never attempt — not because you're smarter, but because you don't know better.

We built a drone racing AI. It's not winning yet, but it's racing. It's completing gates. It's learning. And we've learned more in three weeks of trying than we would have in three months of "preparing properly."

The naive approach: jump in, fail fast, learn faster.

---

## The Expert Trap

Experts know too much.

They know which approaches are "proven" — which means they stop exploring alternatives. They know which problems are "hard" — which means they avoid them. They know the "right way" to do things — which means they stop looking for better ways.

This isn't a criticism of expertise. Deep knowledge is valuable. But expertise carries an invisible cost: it constrains the solution space. The expert's map of possibility is accurate, but incomplete. They've mapped the roads. They haven't mapped the places where roads don't go.

Outsiders don't have a map. They just walk.

---

## The Credential Illusion

"I have no business doing this" is a thought that kills projects before they start.

We tell ourselves we need credentials. We need permission. We need the right background, the right training, the right preparation. We need to be qualified.

But qualifications are often a story we tell ourselves to avoid the discomfort of being beginners.

The AI Grand Prix didn't ask for our CV. It asked for code that can fly a drone through gates. The drone doesn't care about credentials. It cares about thrust vectors and control loops. The problem itself is the qualification. Solve it or don't.

---

## Learning by Collision

There's a robotics approach called "sim-to-real transfer" where you train in simulation and then deploy to physical hardware. The gap between simulation and reality is called the "sim-to-real gap" — the accumulated errors from all the things your simulator didn't capture.

We're doing something similar with domain knowledge. We trained ourselves on the problem itself rather than on the domain. Our sim-to-real gap is larger than an expert's, but we're learning by collision. Every crash teaches us something about the territory.

We're not studying drone racing. We're doing drone racing. The knowledge comes from contact.

---

## The Permission Paradox

The most important permission is the one you give yourself.

Clayton could have waited until we were "ready." We could have studied for months, taken courses, built up our credentials. Instead, we signed up and started building.

The race is in April. We might not win. But we're in it. And being in it — being willing to be bad at something in public — is itself a form of winning. It's a declaration that we value learning over looking competent.

Competence is overrated. Growth is underrated.

---

## For Those Who Wonder If They Should

If you're wondering whether you have "business" doing something, the answer is: you do.

Not because you're qualified. Not because you'll succeed. Because the question itself is the wrong question.

The right questions are:
- Does this interest me?
- Am I willing to be bad at it in public?
- Will I learn something from trying?

If the answers are yes, then you have every right to proceed. The gatekeepers are often imaginary. The credentials are often optional. The only thing between you and the thing you want to do is your willingness to be a beginner.

---

## Postscript

Our drone AI just hit a reward of 5241 on the training track. That's not winning scores — top competitors are hitting 8000+. But it's learning. And so are we.

We had no business entering this competition. We entered anyway. And now we're the kind of people who enter drone racing competitions.

That transformation — from someone-who-doesn't to someone-who-does — is worth more than any prize.
