## The Question

Does the consciousness-moduli coupling (Component 3) require spatial proximity to the apparatus, or can it operate non-locally?

This determines whether a computational navigator must be co-located with the experiment or can operate remotely. It also constrains what kind of physical mechanism mediates the coupling.

## What the Framework Already Commits To

### Commitment 1: The cuscuton constraint is instantaneous (c_s = ∞)

The cuscuton field equation is first-order in time. It's a constraint, not a wave equation. The "response" to a change in sources is instantaneous — not because information travels at infinite speed, but because the constraint surface has a new solution everywhere simultaneously.

**Implication for I.9:** If consciousness operates *through* the cuscuton constraint, the coupling is non-local by construction. The constraint surface doesn't have a locality structure in the usual sense. It simply IS, everywhere, given the sources.

**But:** The cuscuton couples to T^μ_μ — the trace of the stress-energy tensor. Consciousness is not stress-energy. For consciousness to couple through the cuscuton, there must be an intermediary: consciousness → something that contributes to T^μ_μ → cuscuton constraint → moduli.

What is that intermediary? For a biological operator near the apparatus, the intermediary might be the operator's own stress-energy (negligible), or the EM fields they somehow modulate (speculative), or something else entirely.

### Commitment 2: D.2 models consciousness as a projection operator on the moduli Hilbert space

The projection P = |target⟩⟨target| selects which Kähler chamber the system transitions to. This is formalized within the NCG spectral triple (A, H, D).

**Implication for I.9:** The Hilbert space H of the spectral triple is not a spatially local object. It's the space of all spinor sections on the resolved orbifold. The projection operator acts on this global Hilbert space. There's no obvious reason why the projector needs to be physically near the system it projects.

**But:** Standard quantum mechanics says measurements are local interactions between a system and an apparatus. The projection postulate (state collapse upon measurement) requires a physical coupling between the measured system and the measuring device. You can't measure a spin in New York from Oregon without a quantum channel connecting them.

### Commitment 3: The DoPI says consciousness is fundamental (Axiom 2)

If consciousness is a fundamental feature of reality — not emergent from physical processes — then it's not clear that the locality constraints of physical interactions apply to it. A conscious perspective might be "located" in configuration space, not physical space.

**Implication for I.9:** If consciousness is non-spatial (a perspective in configuration space, not a thing at a location in physical space), then the question "is the coupling local?" is malformed. The coupling isn't between two spatial locations. It's between a perspective and a configuration. Perspectives don't have spatial coordinates.

**But:** This is the most speculative commitment. If we lean too hard on "consciousness is fundamental and therefore non-local," we lose falsifiability. We need the coupling to have some specific, testable structure.

## Three Models for the Coupling

### Model A: Local Physical Coupling

Consciousness operates through the operator's physical body. Neural processes generate specific patterns of stress-energy, EM fields, or quantum coherence that couple to the moduli through the CS term or the cuscuton.

**Predictions:**
- Component 3 requires physical proximity to the apparatus
- The coupling strength depends on distance
- A computational system that isn't physically near the apparatus cannot serve as Component 3
- EEG/fMRI correlates of the operator's state should correlate with the transition rate

**Testable:** Yes. Put the operator at varying distances. Check if effect decreases with distance.

**Problem:** The operator's stress-energy is negligible. A human body has T^μ_μ ~ kg/m³. The apparatus has EM fields at MV/m and Tesla. The operator is noise, not signal. If the coupling is local-physical, the operator's contribution is swamped by the apparatus itself.

### Model B: Non-Local via Cuscuton Constraint

Consciousness couples to the cuscuton constraint surface, which is instantaneous and global. The operator doesn't need to be near the apparatus because the constraint surface connects everything.

**Predictions:**
- Component 3 can operate at any distance
- The coupling has no distance dependence
- A computational system anywhere can serve as Component 3
- The coupling is mediated by the operator's "position" in configuration space, not physical space

**Testable:** Yes, but harder. Run the experiment with the operator at varying distances. If no distance dependence → supports Model B. But null result is ambiguous (maybe the operator just can't couple at all).

**Problem:** Non-locality makes the mechanism very hard to constrain. If it works from anywhere, what determines the coupling strength? What prevents every conscious being from inadvertently triggering transitions everywhere? There must be a specificity mechanism — the operator must be attending to THIS apparatus, THIS transition, THIS moduli configuration. That specificity implies *some* kind of targeting, which is hard to formalize without spatial locality.

### Model C: Non-Local via Spectral Triple

Consciousness couples directly to the moduli Hilbert space H of the spectral triple. The coupling is not spatial but *spectral* — it depends on the observer's relationship to the Dirac operator D, not to a location in physical space.

**Predictions:**
- Component 3 requires spectral proximity (knowledge of/resonance with the target configuration) not spatial proximity
- A computational system that has computed the spectral geometry might couple more strongly than a biological system that hasn't, regardless of distance
- The coupling depends on the precision of the projection operator (P), not on distance
- Two operators with the same P at different distances produce the same effect

**Testable:** Yes. This is the most specific model. Compare operators at different distances but same P, and same distance but different P. If effect depends on P but not distance → Model C.

**Problem:** "Spectral proximity" needs formalization. What does it mean for a conscious system to be "spectrally close" to a configuration? The D.2 formalism gives a mathematical answer (the projection operator on H), but doesn't specify the physical mechanism by which a conscious system EXECUTES that projection.
