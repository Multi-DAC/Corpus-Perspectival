### Summary

We propose a simple modification to a standard NMR experiment: applying a static electric
field parallel to the spectrometer's magnetic field and measuring whether any anomalous
NMR response occurs. The modification requires only an electrode insert (~$200 in materials)
and a benchtop HV power supply (~$200-$800). Total cost under $2,000. No modifications to
the spectrometer itself.

The experiment tests a specific prediction from a theoretical framework based on Randall-Sundrum
warped geometry combined with noncommutative spectral geometry. The prediction: a static E·B
field configuration inside a superconducting solenoid creates conditions that can probe
transitions between distinct vacuum configurations of the internal (compactified) geometry.

**Expected result for this phase: null.** We predict no anomalous signal without additional
conditions. The purpose is to establish a clean baseline and demonstrate the apparatus works.
Anomalous results would be unexpected and would warrant further investigation.

### The Experiment

**Setup:** Standard ¹H NMR of water (or other reference sample) in a 5 mm tube fitted
with two platinum disc electrodes separated by 2-5 mm. A DC voltage (1-5 kV) is applied
between the electrodes, creating an electric field parallel to B₀.

**Protocol:**
1. Baseline: Standard ¹H spectrum, E = 0 (multiple acquisitions for statistics)
2. E-field on: Same acquisition with 1 kV applied
3. E-field off: Repeat baseline to check for hysteresis
4. Ramp: Repeat at 2 kV, 3 kV, 5 kV (if probe holds voltage)
5. Relaxation: Measure T₁ and T₂ at each voltage

**Observables:** Chemical shift, linewidth, relaxation times, signal intensity.

**Expected result:** No change beyond known E-field effects (which are negligible for nuclear
transitions at these energies). Any anomaly exceeding the noise floor would be scientifically
interesting regardless of its origin.

### Risk Assessment

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| Electrode causes NMR artifact | Compare E on/off; artifact is frequency-independent |
| HV arc damages probe | Series current-limiting resistor; start at low voltage |
| No interesting result | Expected! Establishes baseline for future phases |
| Theoretical framework is wrong | Experiment is still valid NMR science |

**This experiment cannot damage the spectrometer.** The electrode assembly is a passive
insert. The HV supply is externally current-limited to < 1 mA. The magnetic field is
unaffected by the E field. The worst-case scenario is a blown fuse in the HV supply.
