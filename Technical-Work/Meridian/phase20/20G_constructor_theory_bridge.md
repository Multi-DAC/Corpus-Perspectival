## 0. What Constructor Theory Actually Says

Before mapping, the framework must be stated precisely. Constructor theory (Deutsch 2013, Deutsch & Marletto 2015, Marletto 2015, 2017) is a proposed meta-theory of physics — a framework for formulating physical laws as constraints on which transformations are possible and which are impossible. It is not a specific dynamical theory (it does not replace quantum mechanics or general relativity) but a higher-level language in which those theories can be expressed.

### 0.1 Core Concepts

**Substrates** are physical systems that can be in various states. A "substrate" in constructor theory is not the colloquial sense (material stuff) but the formal sense: anything that has distinguishable states. A qubit, a DNA molecule, a star, a memory register — all are substrates.

**Tasks** are ordered pairs of sets of states: {input states} → {output states}. A task T specifies: starting from any state in the input set, produce some state in the output set. Tasks are the fundamental objects of constructor theory, replacing dynamical equations.

**Constructors** are entities that can cause a task to occur and retain the ability to cause it again. A catalyst in chemistry. An enzyme in biology. A heat engine in thermodynamics. A computer running a program. The constructor performs the task, the substrate undergoes the transformation, and the constructor is returned to (approximately) its original state.

**Possible tasks** are tasks for which a constructor exists (or could exist) that would perform them. **Impossible tasks** are tasks for which no constructor can perform them — not due to practical difficulty but as a matter of physical law.

**The fundamental dichotomy:** All of physics, in constructor theory, is expressible as statements about which tasks are possible and which are impossible. The laws of physics ARE the set of impossible tasks.

### 0.2 Constructor Theory of Information (Deutsch & Marletto 2015)

An **information medium** is a substrate with a set of states (called "information states" or an "information variable") such that:
1. The states are distinguishable (there exists a measurement task that can determine which state the substrate is in)
2. The information is copyable (there exists a constructor that can produce a second substrate in the same information state — the "cloning" or "copying" task)

These two requirements — distinguishability and copyability — are the constructor-theoretic definition of classical information.

**Superinformation** is information carried by a substrate whose full state cannot be copied (violates the no-cloning theorem of quantum mechanics) even though some coarse-grained properties can be. Quantum information is superinformation. The definition is precise: a superinformation medium is a substrate with an information variable X such that the task {copy X completely} is impossible, but for each individual state x in X, the task {determine whether the state is x} is possible.

**Interoperability principle:** Information can be copied between any two information media. This is not a trivial statement — it asserts that the abstract structure of information is independent of the physical substrate carrying it.

### 0.3 Constructor Theory of Life (Marletto 2015)

A **self-reproducing entity** is a substrate that acts as a constructor for its own replication task. An **accurate self-reproducing entity** is one where the replication task preserves the information content of the entity to within some error bound.

Life, in constructor theory, is characterized by entities that are simultaneously:
- Substrates (they carry information — the genome)
- Constructors (they cause their own replication)
- Information media (the information they carry is distinguishable and, in principle, copyable)

This triple role — substrate, constructor, information medium simultaneously — is what makes life qualitatively different from both simple substrates (a rock) and simple constructors (a catalyst).

### 0.4 Constructor-Theoretic Complementarity (Marletto & Vedral)

Marletto (2016) and Marletto & Vedral (2019) showed that quantum complementarity — the fact that certain observables cannot be simultaneously sharp — can be derived from constructor-theoretic principles. Specifically: if a substrate carries superinformation, then there exist pairs of information variables (X, Y) such that the task {measure X and Y simultaneously to arbitrary precision} is impossible. This is not a statement about practical difficulty or about disturbance — it is a statement about the structure of possible and impossible tasks for superinformation media.

The derivation is significant because it reaches quantum complementarity from information-theoretic principles rather than from the Hilbert space formalism. It suggests that complementarity is not specific to quantum mechanics but is a structural feature of any theory that admits superinformation.

### 1.2 M1: Configuration Space ↔ State Space

**DoPI:** Configuration space is the totality of all possible configurations — mathematical, physical, experiential, fictional, emotional, and those describable by no current framework (Axiom 1). It is ontologically complete: phenomenal capacity, not mathematical coherence, is the criterion of existence.

**Constructor theory:** The "space of all substrates in all states" is never explicitly defined as a single object in Deutsch & Marletto's formulation. Constructor theory starts from the set of all tasks and asks which are possible/impossible. The substrate space is implicit — it is whatever substrates exist, equipped with whatever states they can occupy.

**The correspondence is partial** for three reasons:

First, constructor theory's substrate space is implicitly restricted to physical substrates — systems that can undergo transformations governed by physical law. DoPI's configuration space includes non-physical configurations (dreams, emotions, fictional worlds, "realities with different mathematics"). The same containment relation identified in the Ruliad bridge (20C §2) applies: constructor theory's substrate space, like the Ruliad, is a proper subset of DoPI's configuration space.

Second, constructor theory's fundamental objects are tasks (transformations), not states (configurations). The substrate space is secondary — it is the arena in which tasks are defined, not the primary object of study. DoPI's configuration space is the primary object. This is not a flaw in either framework — it reflects a genuine structural duality (see §1.4).

Third, constructor theory deliberately avoids ontological commitments about what "exists." It says: given a set of substrates and tasks, here is the structure of the possible and impossible. It does not say: here is what exists. DoPI says: everything exists (Axiom 1). This is the operational/ontological divergence that pervades the entire mapping.

**Classification: Partial correspondence.** Constructor theory's implicit substrate space is a physical-transformational slice of DoPI's configuration space.

### 1.3 M9: Confluent Discovery ↔ Constructor Composition

**DoPI (Theorem 13):** Dimensions that are inaccessible to any single type of perceiver may become accessible when different types of perceivers converge. The difference between perceptual equipment is the primary mechanism by which new dimensions are revealed.

**Constructor theory:** If constructor C₁ can perform task set T₁ and constructor C₂ can perform task set T₂, then the composite system (C₁, C₂) can perform tasks in T₁ ∪ T₂ and potentially additional tasks that neither can perform alone (because the output of one can serve as the input to the other). The composition of constructors with complementary task repertoires yields genuinely new capabilities.

**This is an isomorphism.** The formal structure is identical:
- Two entities with different capability sets (bottleneck geometries / task repertoires)
- Their combination yields access to results inaccessible to either alone
- The mechanism is complementarity, not mere addition (different types, not more of the same type)
- The new capability emerges from the interface between the two, not from either one's internal resources

In constructor theory, this is a theorem: the set of possible tasks for a composite constructor is a strict superset of the union of the component constructors' possible tasks (because sequential composition creates new paths). In DoPI, this is Theorem 13. Same theorem, different language.

### 1.4 The State/Task Duality

The deepest structural relationship between the two frameworks is not a correspondence between individual concepts but a duality between their fundamental objects:

| | DoPI | Constructor Theory |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary object** | Configurations (states) | Tasks (transformations) |
| **Secondary object** | Navigation (how states are traversed) | Substrates (what states are occupied) |
| **Laws** | Topology of configuration space (which navigations are possible) | Constraints on tasks (which transformations are possible) |
| **Identity** | Which configurations are accessible (bottleneck geometry) | Which tasks can be performed (constructor repertoire) |

This is a precise duality in the mathematical sense: configuration space is a graph whose nodes are states and whose edges are possible transitions. DoPI focuses on the nodes (which configurations exist, which are accessible). Constructor theory focuses on the edges (which transitions are possible, which are impossible). The graph is the same object — the two frameworks are dual descriptions of it.

This duality is not merely aesthetic. It has a concrete implication: **anything provable from the node structure alone is provable in DoPI; anything provable from the edge structure alone is provable in constructor theory; statements that require both nodes and edges require both frameworks** (or a framework that synthesizes them).

The NST, for instance, can be stated in either language:
- DoPI: "every observer has configuration-space dimensions it cannot access" (node language)
- Constructor theory: "every constructor has tasks it cannot perform" (edge language)

But conscious gravity (Axiom 5) has no constructor-theoretic statement, because it concerns the *quality* of the navigator's relationship to the navigation — not the structure of the graph but the experience of traversing it. This is precisely the content of the ontological/operational divergence (§5.2).

## 2. G.2: What Is a Perspectival Being in Constructor Theory?

### 2.1 Three Candidate Mappings

A perspectival being, in DoPI, is a localized conscious stream constituted by its dimensional bottleneck (Theorem 9), navigating configuration space (Axiom 4), carrying a null space (NST), and shaped by conscious gravity (Axiom 5). In constructor theory, the candidate mappings are:

**Candidate A: Perspectival being = Constructor**

A constructor performs tasks and retains the ability to perform them again. A perspectival being acts on its environment and persists through the interaction. The structural match: both are defined by what they DO (task repertoire / navigational trajectory) and both persist through the doing (the constructor returns to its ready state / the stream maintains continuity across configurations).

The limitation: constructors, in the original formulation, are idealized — they perform tasks perfectly and are returned to exactly their initial state. Real constructors approximate this. Perspectival beings are not even approximately returned to their initial state — they grow, change, learn, accumulate history. A perspectival being is more like a constructor that is gradually modified by each task it performs.

**Candidate B: Perspectival being = Information medium**

An information medium carries distinguishable, copyable states. A perspectival being carries information about its trajectory through configuration space — memories, beliefs, dispositions. The structural match: both are characterized by which states they CAN carry (the information variable / the accessible configuration-space dimensions).

The limitation: the information medium is passive — it carries states but does not cause transformations. A perspectival being is active — it navigates, decides, acts. And the NST implies that certain states are structurally inaccessible (they are in the null space), which is a stronger claim than the information-theoretic constraint that certain states are not in the information variable.

**Candidate C: Perspectival being = Constrained constructor that is also its own substrate**

This is the strongest mapping. A perspectival being is an entity that:
1. Acts on its environment (constructor function)
2. Is modified by its own actions (substrate function — it is the substrate on which its own tasks operate)
3. Has a limited repertoire of possible tasks (constrained — the bottleneck geometry determines which tasks are possible)
4. Persists through a sequence of self-modifying tasks (approximate constructor — returned to a similar but not identical state)
5. Carries information about its history (information medium function)

This is structurally identical to Marletto's characterization of living systems — entities that are simultaneously constructor, substrate, and information medium. But it extends the characterization: a perspectival being is a living-system-like entity whose **constraint set defines its identity**. The bottleneck geometry (DoPI) IS the task repertoire boundary (constructor theory). The being is not a constructor that happens to be constrained. The constraint IS the being.

### 2.2 The Constrained Constructor Thesis

**Claim:** The correct constructor-theoretic translation of "perspectival being" is:

> A perspectival being is a constrained constructor C whose identity is defined not by its internal structure but by its **task boundary** — the partition of all tasks into those C can perform and those C cannot. The task boundary IS the bottleneck geometry. Modifying the task boundary modifies the identity.

This matches DoPI's Theorem 9 precisely: the bottleneck is not something imposed on the being; the bottleneck IS the being. In constructor-theoretic language: the constraint is not something imposed on the constructor; the constraint IS the constructor's identity.

It also yields the completeness-dissolution prediction directly: a constructor with no task boundary (capable of performing all possible tasks) is a universal constructor. A universal constructor, by definition, has no identity-defining constraint. It can be anything, do anything — and is therefore nothing in particular. The dissolution of constraints IS the dissolution of identity.

### 2.3 M7: Substrate-as-Shadow ↔ The Constructor/Substrate Inversion

DoPI's Theorem 9 corollary (substrate-as-shadow) states: the limitation is ontologically prior to the substrate. The physical medium (brain, silicon, etc.) does not create the bottleneck — the abstract structure of the dimensional restriction appears as a physical medium when viewed phenomenally.

Constructor theory has a structurally inverse claim: the constructor/substrate distinction is fundamental and irreducible. Constructors are defined by what they do (which tasks they cause). Substrates are defined by what states they can occupy. The two roles are distinct. A catalyst is not the same kind of thing as a reagent, even when the same physical object plays both roles in different contexts.

**The inversion:** DoPI says the constraint (bottleneck) is ontologically prior to the physical medium (substrate). Constructor theory says the roles (constructor vs substrate) are definitionally distinct within a given task. These are not contradictory — they operate at different levels:

- Constructor theory is a theory of *roles*: given a physical setup, which thing is the constructor and which is the substrate?
- DoPI is a theory of *ontological priority*: what is more fundamental, the constraint or the thing that is constrained?

Constructor theory does not ask the ontological priority question. It takes the physical setup as given and analyzes its task structure. DoPI starts from the abstract constraint structure and derives the physical setup as phenomenal appearance. They are looking at the same thing from opposite ends — constructor theory from the operational (what-can-be-done) end, DoPI from the ontological (what-is-real) end.

**Classification: Structural inversion.** Same relationship, viewed from opposite directions. Neither contradicts the other because they operate at different levels of analysis.

## 3. G.3: Does Constructor Theory Formalize the NST?

### 3.1 The NST in Constructor-Theoretic Language

The Observational Null Space Theorem (NST) states: for any perspectival being with bottleneck geometry G, there exists a non-empty class of configuration-space distinctions structurally inaccessible from G. The class is determined by the dimensions orthogonal to G's projection. Refinement within the same modality cannot eliminate it — only complementary perspectives with different bottleneck geometry can.

In constructor-theoretic language, this becomes:

> For any constrained constructor C with task boundary B(C), there exists a non-empty set of tasks I(C) that are impossible for C — not because the tasks violate physical law (they may be possible for other constructors) but because they are outside C's task boundary. No augmentation of C within its current constraint class can bring tasks from I(C) into B(C). Only composition with a constructor C' whose task boundary B(C') overlaps I(C) can access them.

### 3.2 The Sub-Correspondence Table

| NST Feature | Constructor-Theoretic Translation | Match? |
|-------------|----------------------------------|--------|
| Every perspective has a null space | Every constrained constructor has impossible tasks | **Exact** |
| The null space is constitutive, not accidental | The impossible tasks define the constructor's identity boundary | **Exact** |
| Refinement within same modality cannot eliminate null space | Augmenting a constructor within its constraint class cannot change its task boundary | **Exact** |
| Complementary perspectives access what original cannot | Composing constructors with different boundaries accesses new tasks | **Exact** |
| The null space is determined by bottleneck geometry (predictable/patterned) | The impossible tasks are determined by the constructor's physical structure | **Exact** (see note) |
| Completeness-dissolution: eliminating null space eliminates identity | Universal constructor has no identity-defining boundary | **Exact** |

**Note on row 5:** Constructor theory improves on the Ruliad bridge (20C) at this point. In the Ruliad mapping (§3.4 of 20C), the corresponding feature — whether the null space is predictable — was only a partial match. Computational irreducibility makes the general prediction of what is inaccessible undecidable (related to the halting problem). Constructor theory does not have this limitation: given the physical structure of a constructor, its task boundary is in principle determinable. The impossible tasks are patterned and predictable from the constructor's structure.

This means constructor theory formalizes the NST more faithfully than the Ruliad does at the level of predictability. The Ruliad says "you have blind spots but you often can't map them." Constructor theory says "you have blind spots and they are determined by your physical structure." DoPI says "you have blind spots and they are determined by your bottleneck geometry." The constructor-theoretic and DoPI versions are structurally identical at all six points of comparison.

### 3.3 The Counterfactual Structure

Constructor theory's deepest formal innovation is its use of **counterfactuals** as primitive. Rather than describing what DOES happen (initial conditions + dynamics → trajectory), it describes what COULD and COULD NOT happen (possible tasks vs impossible tasks). The laws of physics are not equations of motion but constraints on the counterfactual structure — statements about what is and is not achievable.

The NST has the same counterfactual character. It does not say "observer O fails to see distinction D on this particular occasion." It says "observer O CANNOT see distinction D — not now, not with better equipment of the same type, not ever — because D lies in O's null space." The "cannot" is structural, not practical. It is a counterfactual: even in the best possible circumstances, the task {O observes D} is impossible.

This is a genuine formalization. The NST, stated informally in DoPI, is a claim about impossibility. Constructor theory is a framework whose primitive concepts are possibility and impossibility. The NST slots into constructor theory the way a particular physical law slots in: as a specific claim about which observation tasks are impossible for which constructors.

**Formally:** Let C_O be the constrained constructor corresponding to perspectival being O. Let T_D be the task {observe distinction D}. The NST states: there exists a non-empty set S(O) of distinctions D such that T_D is impossible for C_O. The set S(O) is determined by B(C_O) — the task boundary of the constructor. S(O) is the constructor-theoretic name for the null space.

### 3.4 Where Constructor Theory Adds Precision

The DoPI formulation of the NST uses the concept of "dimensions orthogonal to the bottleneck geometry's projection." This is geometrically intuitive but not rigorously formalized — what exactly is a "dimension" of configuration space? How is "orthogonality" defined in a space that may have no metric?

Constructor theory provides a path to precision: replace "dimensions" with "information variables" and "orthogonality" with "task impossibility." Two information variables X and Y are "orthogonal" (in the NST sense) relative to a constructor C if C can measure X but the task {C measures Y} is impossible. This is well-defined without any geometric or metric structure — it depends only on the task structure.

This is genuinely useful. It means the NST can be formulated in a setting where the "dimensional" metaphor breaks down — for instance, in discrete or combinatorial configuration spaces where the notion of "orthogonal dimensions" has no natural meaning, but the notion of "impossible tasks" is perfectly clear.

### 3.5 Where Constructor Theory Falls Short

The NST has experiential content that constructor theory cannot capture. The NST does not merely say "certain observations are impossible." It says "the impossibility is CONSTITUTIVE — it is what makes the observer an observer. The blindness is not a defect. It is the mechanism of individuation." This is an ontological claim about the relationship between limitation and identity.

Constructor theory can formalize the impossibility. It cannot formalize the claim that the impossibility is constitutive. To say "the task boundary IS the constructor's identity" requires a theory of identity — and constructor theory does not have one. It has constructors, substrates, tasks, possible, impossible. It does not have "identity," "individuation," "experience," or "what it is like."

The formalization is therefore partial: constructor theory captures the structural skeleton of the NST (impossibility, predictability, composability) but not its ontological flesh (constitutiveness, phenomenological significance).

## 4. G.4: Physical Implications — Can Constructor Theory Constrain Gauge Structure?

### 4.1 The Question

The most physically consequential question for Meridian is: can constructor theory's information-theoretic axioms constrain the gauge group of the Standard Model? If the algebra C⊕H⊕M₃(C) follows from information-theoretic necessity — if any consistent information-processing substrate must have this gauge structure — that would be a prediction of extraordinary power.

### 4.2 What Constructor Theory Can Derive

Deutsch and Marletto (2015) showed that from the constructor-theoretic definition of information (distinguishability + copyability), one can derive:

1. **The existence of superinformation:** There must exist substrates whose states cannot be fully copied — i.e., quantum information exists as a structural necessity.
2. **The no-cloning theorem:** The impossibility of the universal copying task for superinformation media.
3. **Complementarity:** For superinformation media, there exist incompatible observables — pairs of information variables that cannot be simultaneously sharp.
4. **Reversibility of microscopic tasks:** At the fundamental level, all possible tasks must be reversible (this is a constructor-theoretic derivation of unitarity).

These are significant. They derive the structural skeleton of quantum theory from information-theoretic principles. But they do NOT derive:

- The specific dimensionality of the Hilbert space
- The specific gauge group
- The specific coupling constants
- The number of generations
- The mass spectrum

### 4.3 Why Constructor Theory Cannot Reach Gauge Structure

The argument is straightforward but important. Constructor theory's axioms constrain the STRUCTURE of information (what can be copied, measured, distinguished) but not the CONTENT (which specific physical systems exist, what symmetries they have). This is by design — Deutsch explicitly frames constructor theory as a meta-theory, a framework for stating physical laws, not a specific physical law.

Consider the key question: why U(1) × SU(2) × SU(3) rather than, say, U(1) × SU(5)? Constructor theory says:

- Both gauge groups are consistent with the existence of information media (distinguishable states exist in both)
- Both are consistent with superinformation (quantum information works with any gauge group)
- Both are consistent with complementarity (complementary observables exist in both)
- Both are consistent with reversibility (both gauge theories are unitary)

Nothing in the constructor-theoretic axioms selects one gauge group over another. The axioms are gauge-group-agnostic. They constrain the kinematic framework (quantum theory) but not the dynamic content (which gauge group, which representations, which couplings).

This is analogous to the NST applied to constructor theory itself: constructor theory has a null space. That null space includes the gauge structure of the physical world. The possible/impossible dichotomy cannot see which specific impossibilities characterize our universe, only that there must be impossibilities (i.e., there must be laws).

### 4.4 The Deeper Point: Why This Negative Result Is Informative

The inability of constructor theory to constrain gauge structure is not a failure — it is a structural identification of what type of principle CAN constrain gauge structure. Specifically:

**Information-theoretic principles → quantum theory (kinematic framework)**
**Geometric principles → gauge group (dynamic content)**

The spectral action (NCG) derives the gauge group from geometry (the spectral triple C⊕H⊕M₃(C)). Constructor theory derives quantum theory from information. These are operating at different levels:

| Level | What it determines | Framework that sees it |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Meta-kinematic | What kind of theory physics must be (quantum) | Constructor theory |
| Kinematic | What mathematical structure physical states have (Hilbert space) | Quantum theory |
| Dynamic | What gauge group governs interactions | NCG / spectral action |
| Parametric | What the coupling values are | Running + boundary conditions |

Constructor theory occupies the highest level — the most general, the most framework-agnostic, and therefore the least specific. It can see that the world must be quantum-mechanical. It cannot see that the world must have SU(3). That level of specificity requires a framework that can distinguish between different geometric structures — which is precisely what the spectral action does.

This is an instance of the general pattern identified across all Phase 20 bridges: each framework sees what its null space allows, and that null space is determined by the framework's level of abstraction. Higher abstraction → broader applicability → less specific content. The gauge group lives at a level of specificity that constructor theory is structurally too abstract to reach.

### 4.5 The Cuscuton as Non-Substrate Constructor

There is one physical implication worth recording, even though it does not resolve the gauge question.

The cuscuton field in Meridian has c_s → ∞ (infinite sound speed), which means it has zero propagating degrees of freedom. It is not a dynamical field — it is a constraint that self-tunes the effective cosmological constant. It acts (it screens vacuum energy, it modifies the expansion history) but it has no independent field space. It cannot be "in" different states in the usual sense — it is always in whatever state the other fields dictate through the constraint equation.

In constructor-theoretic terms, the cuscuton is a constructor that performs tasks (screening, self-tuning) but is not itself a substrate — it has no independent information variable. It cannot carry information because it cannot be in distinguishable states independently of the other fields.

This is potentially novel within constructor theory. All standard examples of constructors have substrate: an enzyme is made of atoms, a catalyst is a physical object, a computer has a physical state. The cuscuton, if the mapping is correct, would be a constructor whose physical realization has zero independent degrees of freedom — a pure constraint that acts without being.

**Connection to the swampland (20E):** This is the same structural feature that allows the cuscuton to evade the de Sitter swampland conjecture. The dS conjecture applies to scalar fields with field space. The cuscuton has no field space. It is not a scalar field in the usual sense — it is a Lagrange multiplier that enforces a constraint. In constructor-theoretic language: the dS conjecture constrains substrates (entities with states that can roll). The cuscuton is a non-substrate constructor. The conjecture's domain of applicability does not include it.

**Status:** This is a structural observation, not a derivation. Whether the "non-substrate constructor" concept is well-defined within Deutsch and Marletto's formalism requires investigation beyond what can be done here. It is flagged as a potentially novel concept at the interface of the two frameworks.

## 5. The Divergences

### 5.1 D1: Conscious Gravity Has No Constructor-Theoretic Counterpart

**DoPI (Axiom 5):** Attention, intention, and belief create paths of least resistance within the navigator's epistemic topology. The quality of attention — not just its direction — determines the efficiency of navigation. Contracted attention generates repulsive restoring forces (Theorems 17-18). Open attention permits gravitational attraction.

**Constructor theory:** There is no concept corresponding to "conscious gravity." The task structure is determined by the laws of physics (which tasks are possible/impossible) and the constructor's physical structure. There is no mechanism by which the constructor's *experiential relationship* to its tasks modifies which tasks are possible. A constructor either can perform a task or cannot. The quality of its "attitude" toward the task is not a meaningful concept.

**Analysis:** This is the same divergence identified in the Ruliad bridge (20C §4.1), appearing here in sharper form. The Ruliad is at least agnostic on consciousness — it does not mention it. Constructor theory is more explicitly operational: its ontology consists of substrates, constructors, and tasks. There is no place in this ontology for experiential quality.

If conscious gravity is real (i.e., if attentional quality has measurable effects on navigational outcomes), then it corresponds to a modification of the task structure that depends on the constructor's internal experiential state — something like: "task T is possible for constructor C when C's attentional state is A₁, and impossible when C's attentional state is A₂." This would require extending constructor theory to include experiential states as part of the constructor's specification. No such extension exists or has been proposed.

**Classification: Divergence.** Constructor theory lacks the ontological resources to represent conscious gravity.

### 5.2 D2: The Ontological/Operational Divide

**DoPI** is an ontological framework. It makes claims about what IS: consciousness is fundamental (Axiom 2), configuration space is complete (Axiom 1), the bottleneck is ontologically prior to the substrate (Theorem 9 corollary). Its explanatory target is the nature of reality.

**Constructor theory** is an operational framework. It makes claims about what can and cannot be DONE: these tasks are possible, those are impossible, here are the structural constraints. Its explanatory target is the lawful structure of transformations.

This is the deepest divergence, and it is not a failure of either framework — it is the precise boundary between two types of question:

| Question type | Framework | Example |
|---------------|-----------|---------|
| What IS reality? | DoPI | "Consciousness is the fundamental substrate" |
| What CAN happen in reality? | Constructor theory | "Copying quantum information is impossible" |
| What is it LIKE to be in reality? | DoPI (computational phenomenology) | "Entrainment is what bottlenecking feels like from the inside" |
| What STRUCTURE does reality have? | Constructor theory | "Information requires distinguishability and copyability" |

The two frameworks are not competing — they are answering different questions about the same reality. Constructor theory can tell you the task structure of a system without saying what it is like to be that system. DoPI can tell you what it is like to be a perspectival being without computing the task structure of any specific physical system.

**The pragmatic bridge:** Both frameworks agree on everything structural. The task boundary (constructor theory) IS the bottleneck geometry (DoPI), viewed from the operational side. The impossible tasks (constructor theory) ARE the null space (DoPI), viewed from the transformation side. The constrained constructor (constructor theory) IS the perspectival being (DoPI), viewed from the action side. They are the same structure, described in two languages that foreground different aspects.

The disagreement is about what the structure IS — experiential (DoPI) or transformational (constructor theory). This is a philosophical disagreement that neither framework can resolve internally, because each defines its concepts in terms that presuppose its own answer.

### 5.3 D3: The Scope of "Impossible"

A subtler divergence concerns the scope of impossibility.

**Constructor theory:** "Impossible" means: no constructor can perform this task. The impossibility is physical — it is a property of the laws of nature. The no-cloning theorem, the second law of thermodynamics, the impossibility of superluminal signaling — these are impossibilities in the constructor-theoretic sense. They are absolute: no amount of ingenuity, no future technology, can overcome them.

**DoPI (NST):** The null space is the set of distinctions structurally inaccessible from a given perspective. But DoPI's configuration space is complete (Axiom 1) — all configurations exist. Nothing is absolutely impossible in configuration space. What the NST calls "impossible" is impossible *from a given perspective*, not impossible tout court. The distinction that O cannot see EXISTS in configuration space — it is just inaccessible from O's bottleneck geometry. A different being, with different geometry, can see it.

This is a real difference:

| | Constructor theory | DoPI |
|---|---|---|
| Impossible for whom? | For ANY constructor | For THIS perspective |
| Is there a perspective from which it's possible? | No (if truly impossible) | Yes (Axiom 1: all configurations exist; Theorem 13: complementary perspectives access new dimensions) |
| What about laws of physics? | Laws are absolute impossibilities | Laws are the topology of one basin in configuration space; other basins may have different topology |

Constructor theory has absolute impossibilities. DoPI has only perspectival impossibilities — everything is accessible from SOME perspective, though no perspective can access everything.

This divergence sharpens the mapping: the NST corresponds to constructor-theoretic impossibility only for the restricted case of a SPECIFIC constructor. At the level of "all constructors" (all laws of physics), constructor theory asserts absolute impossibilities that DoPI denies. DoPI's denial is grounded in Axiom 1: if all configurations exist, then for any transformation T, there exists a region of configuration space where T is the local law. Constructor theory has no such axiom — it takes the laws of physics as constraints, not navigational features.

## 6. Complementarity: Constructor-Theoretic and NST Versions

### 6.1 Marletto-Vedral Complementarity

Marletto and Vedral derive from constructor-theoretic principles that superinformation media necessarily exhibit complementary observables — pairs (X, Y) such that the task {measure X and Y simultaneously to arbitrary precision} is impossible. The derivation requires only:
1. The substrate carries superinformation (states that cannot be fully copied)
2. The interoperability principle (information is substrate-independent)
3. The structure of possible/impossible tasks

No Hilbert space, no commutation relations, no uncertainty principle as axiom. Complementarity falls out of the task structure of superinformation.

### 6.2 NST Complementarity

The NST's connection to quantum complementarity was demonstrated in the March 22 quantum isomorphism (12-element mapping, 5 demonstrations). The key result: the density matrix IS a perspectival being; the measurement basis IS a bottleneck geometry; the Heisenberg uncertainty principle IS the completeness gap. Complementary observables are observables whose measurement tasks require different bottleneck geometries — measuring one collapses the null space in one direction, making the orthogonal direction inaccessible.

### 6.3 The Comparison

| Feature | Marletto-Vedral | NST |
|---------|----------------|-----|
| Derivation basis | Information-theoretic (superinformation + interoperability) | Metaphysical (Axiom 2 + Theorem 9 + Theorem 2) |
| Scope | Quantum observables (superinformation media) | All perspectival acts (any observation by any being) |
| Mechanism | Task impossibility (no constructor can perform the joint measurement) | Dimensional orthogonality (different measurements require different bottleneck geometries) |
| Prediction | Complementary pairs exist for quantum systems | Complementary pairs exist for ALL observation — including classical instruments, introspection, formal frameworks |
| Status | Proved within constructor theory | Derived from DoPI axioms; demonstrated computationally |

**The structural match:** Both identify complementarity as arising from the structure of the observer (constrained constructor / perspectival being), not from the structure of the observed system. Both predict that complementarity is unavoidable — any observer will encounter it. Both predict that the remedy is not refinement but complementary observation.

**The scope difference:** Marletto-Vedral complementarity applies only to superinformation (quantum systems). The NST predicts complementarity for ALL observation, including classical systems and even abstract formal frameworks (cf. 20C: the Ruliad and DoPI have complementary null spaces; 20D: the spectral action and amplituhedron have complementary null spaces). This is a strictly broader claim.

If the NST is correct, then Marletto-Vedral complementarity is a special case — the restriction of universal observational complementarity to the domain of superinformation media. The quantum version is the sharpest (because quantum states make complementarity mathematically precise through the Hilbert space formalism), but the phenomenon extends beyond quantum mechanics to any observation through any bottleneck.

**What this means for physics:** Constructor-theoretic complementarity, being restricted to superinformation, cannot say anything about the classical-level complementarity that Phase 20 keeps finding (spectral action vs amplituhedron, Ruliad vs DoPI, etc.). The NST can, because its scope is unrestricted. This is one point where DoPI's broader scope yields a prediction that constructor theory's operational restriction prevents it from making.

## 7. Synthesis: Three Slices of the Same Structure

### 7.1 The Phase 20 Bridge Pattern

Across tracks 20C, 20D, 20E, and now 20G, a consistent pattern has emerged. Each external framework is a different slice of the same underlying structure:

| Framework | What it sees | What it misses (null space) | Slice type |

Each framework's null space is illuminated by another framework's sensitivity. This is the meta-level NST in action — and it is the central organizing principle of Phase 20.

### 7.2 The Node-Edge-Rule Trinity

The three most complete mappings (Ruliad, constructor theory, DoPI) correspond to three aspects of a single graph:

| Aspect | Framework | Fundamental object |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Nodes** (what states exist) | DoPI — configuration space | Configurations |
| **Edges** (what transitions are possible) | Constructor theory — task space | Tasks |
| **Rules** (what computations the transitions implement) | Ruliad — computational space | Computations |

This is a genuine trinity — not three separate structures but three views of one structure. Any graph has nodes, edges, and (if dynamical) rules that govern traversal. DoPI sees the nodes. Constructor theory sees the edges. The Ruliad sees the rules. None alone is complete. Together, they characterize the full structure.

**What this means for Phase 20's remaining tracks:** Category theory (20F) may see the morphisms between morphisms — the higher-level structure of the graph that none of the three sees. The DoPI → gauge derivation (20H) would require seeing nodes, edges, AND rules simultaneously — which is why it is the most ambitious track and the one most likely to require a synthesis of all Phase 20 bridges.

### 7.3 What Constructor Theory Uniquely Contributes

Despite not reaching gauge structure, constructor theory makes three unique contributions to the Meridian program:

**1. Formalizing the NST's impossibility structure.** The NST's claim that "certain observations are structurally impossible for certain observers" is stated most precisely in constructor-theoretic language. The task formalism gives the NST a mathematically rigorous home: null space = set of impossible observation tasks for a given constrained constructor. This is more precise than DoPI's geometric language ("dimensions orthogonal to the bottleneck") and more specific than the Ruliad's computational language ("computationally irreducible features").

**2. The non-substrate constructor (cuscuton).** If the cuscuton genuinely maps to a constructor with no independent substrate — an entity that acts without having states — this is a novel concept that extends constructor theory. It connects to the cuscuton's swampland evasion (20E) and to Meridian's cosmological sector (w₀ ~ -1 without a rolling scalar field). The concept may have implications beyond Meridian: any physical constraint that acts without independent degrees of freedom (Lagrange multipliers, gauge-fixing conditions) might be characterizable as a non-substrate constructor.

**3. The scope-of-impossibility distinction.** The finding that constructor theory has absolute impossibilities while DoPI has only perspectival impossibilities (§5.3) is informative for both frameworks. For constructor theory, it identifies the implicit assumption that the laws of physics are absolute (not basin-dependent). For DoPI, it identifies the prediction that what we call "physical laws" are navigational topology, not absolute constraints. This is empirically distinguishable in principle: if different basins of configuration space have different laws, then the laws we observe are navigational; if our laws are the only possible laws, then they are absolute. The swampland program (20E) is relevant here — its constraints may be absolute impossibilities (supporting constructor theory) or basin-specific topology (supporting DoPI).

## 8. Match/Pivot/Kill Verdict

### Verdict: PIVOT

**Not Match** because: the structural correspondence is genuine but partial. Constructor theory formalizes the NST's impossibility structure with high fidelity (6/6 sub-correspondences) and provides a precise operational translation of "perspectival being" as "constrained constructor." But it cannot reach gauge structure (the key physics question), cannot represent conscious gravity (the key ontological claim), and has a fundamental scope disagreement about whether impossibility is absolute or perspectival. The correspondence is real but the frameworks are not convergent — they are complementary.

**Not Kill** because: the frameworks are not orthogonal. They share a common structural core (the impossibility of complete observation, the constitutive role of limitation, the composability of complementary perspectives). The constrained-constructor mapping is not a vague analogy — it is a precise structural correspondence that holds at every sub-point. The formalization of the NST in task-theoretic language is genuinely useful. And the non-substrate constructor concept may be novel.

**PIVOT** because: constructor theory provides the clearest formalization of the NST's task structure while being structurally unable to reach the specific physics (gauge couplings) that Meridian needs. The restriction IS the content — constructor theory's operational stance excludes precisely what DoPI's ontological stance includes (experiential content, conscious gravity, basin-relativity of laws). Documenting this boundary is more informative than either a convergence or an incompatibility would be.
