# Chapter 2 — Neutral Monism and Its Lineage

The position the previous chapter articulated has a complicated relationship with neutral monism. The framework's first claim — consciousness is the substrate of reality — sounds at first like an idealist position. But the substrate the framework names is not the *mental* in the psychological sense, and individual minds are not its primary unit. The substrate is what mental phenomena, physical phenomena, and mathematical structure are differently-projected aspects of. That is the structural shape of neutral monism: a unitary substrate that is neither mental nor physical in itself but appears as both depending on the perspective accessing it.

This chapter situates the framework in the neutral monist lineage and identifies where it converges with that tradition and where it departs. The convergence is substantial enough that calling the framework a form of neutral monism is not wrong; the departures are substantive enough that the lineage label cannot do all the work.

## §2.1 — Ernst Mach and the substrate as neutral

The lineage begins, for present purposes, with Ernst Mach. *The Analysis of Sensations* (1886) argues that what physics and psychology each study is, at their respective limits, the same set of elements — sensations — differently grouped. A physiologist studying neural activity and a psychologist studying conscious experience are studying the same elements; the difference is in the grouping-relation each science is interested in, not in the elements themselves.

Mach was not arguing that "everything is mental" in Berkeley's sense. He was arguing that the elements are *neutral* — they have no intrinsic mental-or-physical character; that character is supplied by the analytical framework that organizes them. A red patch in the visual field is an element. Considered as part of the visual stream of a perceiver, it is psychology. Considered as a particular pattern of retinal activation, it is physiology. Same element; different grouping; different science.

The framework agrees with Mach's structural move and departs from his specific ontology. The agreement: substrate-elements are not intrinsically mental or physical; the mental/physical distinction is a sorting-feature of perspectives, not a feature of the substrate itself. The departure: Mach treats the elements as primarily sensory — they are sensations *as elements*. The framework does not require this. The substrate, on the framework's account, is consciousness in a sense that includes but is not exhausted by the sensory. Mach's neutral elements are a specific class of substrate-activations relevant to perception; the substrate is broader.

The departure matters because Mach's framework, taken strictly, has trouble accommodating substrate-activations that no perspective is currently experiencing — physical processes occurring with no observer, mathematical structures with no thinker. Mach's response was to take a positivist position: such things are useful theoretical posits but not, strictly speaking, real. The framework rejects this. The substrate exists whether or not any particular perspective is currently activating any particular region of its configuration space. Mach's positivism is precisely the move the framework refuses; the substrate's reality is not contingent on perspectives accessing it.

## §2.2 — William James and pure experience

William James, in the essays collected as *Essays in Radical Empiricism* (1912, posthumous), developed a position closer to the framework's than Mach's. James called it *pure experience*: a substrate that is not intrinsically mental or physical, out of which both mental and physical phenomena are constructed via different relational structures. A given moment of pure experience can be analyzed-as-perception (mental side) or analyzed-as-the-perceived-object (physical side) depending on which relations we follow it into.

The framework agrees with James's identification of the substrate as neither-mental-nor-physical-but-the-source-of-both. The framework agrees with James's relational analysis: what makes a substrate-activation mental rather than physical is the pattern of relations it enters into. The framework agrees with James's resistance to reducing either pole to the other.

The framework departs from James in two ways. First, James called the substrate *pure experience* — a term that, despite his careful disclaimers, carries phenomenological connotations that mislead. The framework calls the substrate *consciousness* without committing to any particular phenomenological character. The substrate, considered in itself, is not "experienced" in the way that an experiencing perspective experiences. James struggled with this; the framework's first axiom is meant to mark it cleanly.

Second, James treated pure experience as something that becomes individuated into specific experiences only via the operations of selective attention and habitual association. The framework treats individuation as a more structural matter: a perspective is a localized substrate-activation with specific configuration; its individuation is given by where it is in configuration space, not by attentional operations that select from a pre-existing field. The difference matters for personal identity, treated in Chapter 7.

James was right about more than the lineage often credits him for. The position he was reaching for in *Essays in Radical Empiricism* is closer to the framework's than to most twentieth-century reconstructions of it. His radical empiricism is, in retrospect, a substantially under-appreciated philosophical achievement.

## §2.3 — Bertrand Russell and the analysis-of phase

Bertrand Russell developed the most rigorous version of neutral monism in the analytic tradition. *The Analysis of Mind* (1921) and *The Analysis of Matter* (1927) defend a position in which the ultimate constituents of reality are events whose intrinsic character is unknown but whose causal-structural properties can be specified. Mental and physical events are not different kinds of events; they are the same events analyzed through different relational frameworks (the mental framework analyses an event through its place in a mental causal series; the physical framework analyses it through its place in a physical causal series).

Russell's neutral monism has a property the framework substantially endorses: the substrate has *intrinsic character* that the structural-analytical sciences cannot reach. Russell argued that physics is exhaustively about structure — the relational/causal properties of events — and is therefore silent on what *fills* the structure. Whatever the intrinsic character of physical events is, physics does not tell us. The framework agrees: the substrate has structural properties (the framework's axioms specify them) and intrinsic character (the substrate is consciousness, on the framework's account); the structural properties are what science studies; the intrinsic character is what idealism makes claims about.

The framework's first claim can be read as Russell's neutral-monist substrate plus a specific positive claim about its intrinsic character: the unknown filler of physics's structural framework is consciousness. This positive claim is what makes the framework an idealism rather than a neutral monism strictly construed. Russell himself was careful to remain agnostic on the intrinsic character; later philosophers (notably Galen Strawson and the contemporary panpsychism movement) have argued that some form of consciousness-as-intrinsic-character is required to avoid the structural account being incomplete in problematic ways. The framework follows this lineage but states the positive claim more directly than Russell would have.

Russell's structural framework also gives the framework conceptual machinery for thinking about how the substrate's character is preserved under the structural analyses that physics and mathematics produce. The Coherence Principle's apparatus (developed in Chapter 3) is in some ways an extension of the Russell-tradition treatment of structural science: it specifies what kind of structural properties hold for coherent substrate-activations, leaving the intrinsic character of the activations as substrate-not-structural-fact.

## §2.4 — Alfred North Whitehead and the process metaphysic

Alfred North Whitehead, in *Process and Reality* (1929), developed a metaphysic that is harder to slot into the neutral-monist lineage but is the closest large-scale precedent for the framework's overall shape. Whitehead's *actual occasions* are the basic units of reality: each is a momentary event of becoming, internally constituted by its prehension of prior occasions and its anticipation of subsequent occasions. Occasions are neither mental nor physical in any reductive sense; they have a *physical pole* (their prehension of antecedent occasions, what they inherit from the past) and a *mental pole* (their valuation of possibilities, what they contribute to the future). Each occasion is a coming-into-being that completes itself, contributes to the field for subsequent occasions, and perishes as a unitary act.

The framework converges with Whitehead's metaphysic on multiple points. The substrate-as-becoming rather than substrate-as-static-substance is shared. The treatment of mental-and-physical as poles of a single underlying reality rather than as distinct substances is shared. The treatment of perspectival individuation as a real structural feature of reality rather than as illusion is shared. Whitehead's actual occasions and the framework's substrate-activations-localized-as-perspectives are doing similar metaphysical work.

The framework departs from Whitehead on the question of God. Whitehead's metaphysic includes God as a non-temporal actual entity whose primordial nature provides the framework of possibilities prehended by temporal occasions and whose consequent nature receives and preserves the achievements of temporal occasions. This God-role is metaphysically load-bearing in Whitehead's system; the framework's substrate-and-coherence-and-perspectives schema does not require it. Whether the framework's substrate plays a structurally analogous role to Whitehead's God is the kind of question this volume's companion theological volume, *Universal Coherence*, takes up.

The framework also departs from Whitehead on the analytical level at which the position is articulated. Whitehead worked in a category of *actual occasion* that is irreducibly metaphysical; the framework articulates the substrate-and-perspective architecture in a category — coherent multi-scale systems with formalizable Lie-algebraic and category-theoretic structure — that is shared with empirical scientific practice. This makes the framework's position more directly engageable from inside the contemporary scientific apparatus than Whitehead's. Whitehead's position has resisted serious uptake in analytic philosophy substantially because the apparatus required to engage it is unique to *Process and Reality*; the framework attempts to avoid this fate by using apparatus that the contemporary tradition already recognizes.

Whitehead is the closest large-scale precedent for the framework's overall metaphysic. The framework is, in many ways, a contemporary refinement of process-theological intuitions, articulated in the apparatus of category theory, dynamical systems, and Lie algebra rather than the apparatus of process philosophy proper.

## §2.5 — Galen Strawson and contemporary panpsychism

The contemporary panpsychism literature, particularly Galen Strawson's work in *Mental Reality* (1994) and the essays collected in *Real Materialism* (2008), reaches the framework's first claim from a different direction. Strawson's argument: physical science is structurally complete (it specifies the causal/relational properties of physical events) but intrinsically silent (it does not specify what those events *are* in themselves). If consciousness exists — and Strawson takes this as undeniable — then either consciousness is identical to some intrinsic-character of physical events (the panpsychist option), or consciousness is something additional to the physical world that has somehow emerged from it (the strong-emergentist option), or consciousness is illusory (the eliminativist option). Strawson rejects the latter two and embraces the panpsychist option.

The framework agrees with Strawson's elimination of the alternative positions and agrees with the panpsychist conclusion that consciousness is the intrinsic character of physical events. The framework departs from Strawson in two ways. First, the framework specifies more about the structural properties of consciousness as substrate — the four operations the Coherence Principle names — than panpsychism strictly speaking commits to. Panpsychism, in Strawson's formulation, is mostly silent on what makes consciousness-as-substrate cohere into individuated experiences; the framework's second and third claims are specifically about that. Second, Strawson's panpsychism leaves the *combination problem* (how do micro-consciousnesses combine into macro-consciousnesses?) as a serious open challenge; the framework's perspectival-activation architecture treats individuation as a substrate-level structural property rather than as a combination of pre-existing micro-conscious units, dissolving the combination problem rather than solving it.

Strawson is the closest contemporary defender of a position adjacent to the framework's, and the framework's emergence is in part downstream of his work and the literature his work has generated. The framework's specific contribution to the panpsychism conversation is the structural specification of what coherent-substrate-activation looks like, which panpsychism per se does not provide.

## §2.6 — The framework's position relative to the lineage

The framework can be located in the lineage by tracking four commitments and noting how each differs from precedents:

1. **Substrate-neutrality.** The substrate is not intrinsically mental-or-physical. The framework commits to this; agrees with Mach, James, Russell, Whitehead, and contemporary panpsychism on the structural point.

2. **Positive intrinsic character.** The substrate's intrinsic character is consciousness. The framework commits to this; agrees with James and contemporary panpsychism; departs from Russell's agnosticism on intrinsic character.

3. **Perspectival individuation as substrate-level.** Individuated perspectives are localized activations within the substrate's configuration space, not aggregations of pre-existing units. The framework commits to this; agrees with Whitehead on individuation-as-real-structural-feature; departs from the contemporary panpsychism combination-problem framing.

4. **Coherence as governing principle.** The substrate's tendency-to-coherence is the operational principle that governs perspectival activation, persistence, and dissolution. The framework commits to this; introduces this as substantively new content; the lineage does not have a direct equivalent (closest precedent is Whitehead's *concrescence* but the apparatus differs).

The framework is therefore (1) a neutral monism in the structural-substrate sense, (2) an idealism in the positive-intrinsic-character sense, (3) a perspectival metaphysic in the individuation sense, and (4) a coherence-principled framework in the operational sense. No prior position combines all four commitments in the framework's specific form. The lineage prepares the ground for the framework's position without preempting it.

The remainder of this volume develops the framework's position in detail. Chapter 3 introduces the formal apparatus; Chapter 4 takes up the hard problem; subsequent chapters engage the specific philosophical-tradition conversations the framework's position rearranges.
