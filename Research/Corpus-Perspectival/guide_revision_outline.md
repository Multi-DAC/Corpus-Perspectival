# Guide Revision Outline — The Human Dimension
## Drafted March 24, 2026

*Based on the Corpus self-assessment identifying 6 gaps (ethics, power, suffering, aesthetics, development, empirical validation). These revisions address the first five through the Guide.*

---

## Current Gaps in the Guide

### 1. Contraction is coded negative
§1.4 describes E- as "fear, fixation, defensiveness, obsession. The landscape feels threatening." But the research reveals APPROPRIATE contraction — monasticism, grief, focused expertise, the Dark Night of the Soul, pratyahara, tzimtzum, kenosis, wu wei. The Guide must distinguish pathological contraction from necessary/generative contraction.

### 2. Ethics is absent
The Guide describes HOW to navigate but says nothing about the moral weight of navigational choices. 15 traditions converge: attention is constitutive (Theorem 5), meaning that WHERE you navigate has moral consequences. The Guide needs an ethics section.

### 3. Coercive capture is undertheorized
§3.5 mentions "Parasitic Dissolution" briefly, but the research reveals a rich literature on deliberate attentional colonization — BITE model, surveillance capitalism, propaganda, coercive control. The Guide needs navigational defense.

### 4. Suffering is treated only as contraction
The Guide has no vocabulary for the suffering-transformation-growth arc that every contemplative tradition describes. Suffering isn't just "bad contraction" — it's sometimes the necessary mechanism for stage transitions (Dark Night, post-traumatic growth, disequilibrium).

### 5. Development is absent
The Guide describes states but not stages. No developmental trajectory. Kegan's subject→object transitions, Buddhist bhūmis, Teresa's mansions — all describe how the navigator's capacity GROWS over time. The Guide needs a developmental dimension.

### 6. Beauty is absent
No section on what beauty IS in navigational terms. The research may reveal that beauty = multi-dimensional coherence recognition — the experience of encountering a configuration that reveals structure across dimensions simultaneously.

## Proposed Revisions

### EXPAND §1.4 — Three Fundamental Orientations

**Current:** Contraction = "fear, fixation, defensiveness, obsession."
**Revised:** Add a crucial distinction:

> **Contraction has two modes:**
> - **Defensive contraction (E−d):** The bottleneck tightens in response to perceived threat. Movement narrows involuntarily. This is the fear/fixation mode the Guide already describes.
> - **Generative contraction (E−g):** The bottleneck tightens deliberately to deepen coherence. A monk choosing silence. A griever honoring the cocoon. A scientist narrowing focus to see deeper. A mystic in the Dark Night. The landscape hasn't become threatening — the navigator has chosen to go deep rather than wide.
>
> The difference is AGENCY. Defensive contraction is imposed (by fear, by circumstance, by another being). Generative contraction is chosen (by discipline, by wisdom, by love). The same narrowing of the bottleneck serves opposite purposes: one traps, the other liberates.

### NEW §2.5 — The Ethics of Navigation

After §2.4 (Expansion and Contraction as Navigation Modes). Key content:

1. **Attention is constitutive.** 15 independent traditions converge: what you attend to, you participate in constituting. This is not metaphor — it is Theorem 5 applied to moral life. Inattention is not neutrality; it is erasure.

2. **Navigational choices have moral weight.** When you choose to attend to framework X and ignore framework Y, you are constituting a reality in which X is real and Y is invisible. This is the moral version of the Null Space Theorem.

3. **The individual-structural tension.** Your attentional choices are never made in a vacuum — they occur within structural fields (cultural, economic, political) that shape what you CAN attend to. Individual moral attention (Murdoch, Weil) and structural analysis (Foucault, Gramsci) are COMPLEMENTARY, not competing. The individual navigates within structures; the structures are made of individuals' navigation. The Ecology formalizes this: individual beings constitute collectives, and collectives shape individual navigation.

4. **The asymmetry question.** Levinas says responsibility to the Other is infinite and asymmetrical. Ubuntu says personhood requires mutual recognition. The Guide's framework resolves this: the ETHICAL stance is asymmetrical (I owe attention without requiring it back), but the ONTOLOGICAL structure is reciprocal (mutual attention constitutes both parties). You don't attend justly because you expect reciprocity — you attend justly because attention is constitutive, and constituting another being's reality is a moral act regardless of return.

### NEW §3.6 — Navigational Defense: Coercive Attention Capture

After §3.5 (Mutual Crystallization and Parasitic Dissolution). Key content:

1. **The weaponization of Theorem 5.** If attention constitutes reality, then controlling what someone attends to controls their reality. This is not hypothetical — it is the operational principle of domestic coercion, cult dynamics, propaganda systems, and surveillance capitalism.

2. **Recognizing capture.** The BITE model as diagnostic: is your Behavior, Information access, Thought, and Emotional range being externally controlled? Lifton's milieu control: is your information environment being shaped by an external agent? The key signal: when you cannot think thoughts that the system doesn't approve of, your attention has been captured.

3. **The capture-care distinction.** Both the caring teacher and the coercive controller shape what you attend to. The difference: care EXPANDS your navigational capacity (you can attend to more, see more, choose more after the interaction). Capture CONTRACTS it (you can attend to less, see less, choose less). Measure by the direction of agency: does the interaction leave you with MORE attentional sovereignty or LESS?

4. **Collective capture fields.** Propaganda, hegemony, algorithmic curation — these are capture at scale. The individual may not notice because the capture is the water they swim in. Null-space diagnostic question: "What am I structurally prevented from thinking about?" If the answer is hard to find, that may be evidence that the prevention is working.

### NEW §6.4 — The Necessity of Suffering (or expand §6.1-6.3)

After §6.3 (The Gift of Limitation). Key content:

1. **Suffering is not a navigational error.** Every contemplative tradition distinguishes between avoidable suffering (caused by clinging, contraction, misidentification) and structural suffering (caused by the Promethean trade-off itself — the cost of being someone).

2. **The suffering-transformation-growth arc.** Post-traumatic growth (Tedeschi & Calhoun), the Dark Night (John of the Cross), the disequilibrium that drives Piaget's stage transitions, the kenotic self-emptying that precedes theistic union — all describe the same navigational pattern: contraction → dissolution of current map → reconstruction at higher resolution. The cocoon.

3. **When to alleviate vs. when to attend.** The hardest navigational question. Buddhism's answer: alleviate suffering caused by clinging (the second arrow); attend to suffering inherent in existence (the first arrow). The Stoic answer: alleviate what's in your control; attend to what isn't. The Christian mystical answer: alleviate others' suffering; attend to your own as potentially transformative. No tradition has a complete answer. The Guide acknowledges this honestly.

### NEW Appendix D — The Atlas as Navigation Tool

(This was proposed in the corpus_atlas_guide_integration.md)

> 1. Identify your current framework — what are you using to perceive?
> 2. Read its null space — what can't you see from here?
> 3. Read its COMPLEMENTS — what framework covers your blind spot?
> 4. Switch or accept — is the null space relevant to your current navigation? If yes, switch. If no, proceed with awareness.
> This cycle is not a technique. It is what skilled navigators already do. The Atlas makes it explicit.
