## What it argues

Mills & Simpson present a model of competition between unicellular and multicellular forms in a spatially varied (heterogeneous) environment. Core claim: **multicellularity can become and remain ecologically stable without direct fitness benefits**, provided the two forms use the varied environment in different ways. The mechanisms are **indirect**:

- **Escape from competition.** The multicellular form isn't "better" — it occupies environmental patches the unicellular form exploits less efficiently, avoiding head-to-head competition.
- **Increased environmental exploitation.** The varied environment, partitioned between the two scales, is exploited more fully than by either scale alone. Both forms persist.

No direct benefit — no greater reproductive rate, no predation resistance, no metabolic efficiency gain — is required. The multi-scale organization is ecologically stable *because* the two scales partition the substrate (the varied environment) differently.

Companion commentary "Direct benefits are not necessary for the evolution of multicellularity" reinforces the frame shift: multicellularity doesn't require fitness-advantage explanations; it requires substrate-heterogeneity-plus-differential-exploitation.

## Where we agree

This is one of the strongest empirical-modelling vindications of a structural Coherence-Principle claim to arrive in our reading register. Three points of agreement:

1. **Coherence is about substrate-organization, not fitness-landscape optimization.** The Principle's multi-scale coherence condition is "scales partition the substrate differently and sustain one another." Mills & Simpson's model IS this condition, operationalised for multicellularity's emergence, with ecological stability as the observable.

2. **Scale-emergence does not require scale-superiority.** The Principle does not predict that a new coherent scale must "outperform" the lower scale. It predicts that the new scale must partition the substrate in a way that sustains itself without disrupting the lower scale's partition. That's exactly the Mills–Simpson niche-partitioning result.

3. **"For free" is the right framing.** In Principle vocabulary: coherent multi-scale structures are not *earned* via fitness advantage — they *arise* when substrate-partitioning conditions obtain. Mills & Simpson give an ecological test case of that arising.

## Where we diverge / where to be careful

- Mills & Simpson model a specific ecological scenario (spatial heterogeneity + two-scale competition). Generalizing to "multi-scale coherence everywhere" is our lift, not theirs. We should cite the paper for the specific claim and not over-extend it.
- The paper's "for free" framing is ecologically specific (no fitness cost, no fitness benefit). Our Principle framing is broader (substrate-partitioning sufficient for coherent persistence). These are compatible but not identical — Living Architecture volume should do the work of showing the mapping carefully, not assume it.
- The paper doesn't claim a general principle; it models a case. We shouldn't write "Mills & Simpson say multi-scale coherence for free is universal" because they don't say that. What they say is: *in this model, in this scenario, no direct benefits are required.*
