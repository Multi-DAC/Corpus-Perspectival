## What it argues

García-Pintos et al. present a theoretical method: given (a) full knowledge of a quantum system's pre-measurement state and (b) the post-measurement outcome, a designed Hamiltonian control sequence can **reverse the measurement exactly** — push the system back to its pre-measurement state and extract the energy the measurement injected. Framed as a Maxwell-demon-style controlled-information result within quantum thermodynamics.

Key properties:
- Does NOT violate the second law. The reversal is local, controlled, and depends on full information.
- Does NOT apply to arbitrary measurements. It applies when the experimenter has the measurement record and can design the inverse Hamiltonian.
- Empirical barrier: current light-collection efficiency ~50%, preventing exact reconstruction. Theoretical barrier does not exist; practical implementation requires much higher measurement precision.
- Applications: energy recovery from measurement events ("store it in a battery"), reversing decoherence in quantum computing.

## The structural pairing with Watanabe-Takagi (load-bearing for us)

Read together with Watanabe & Takagi (universal work extraction without prior state knowledge, Nature Comms 2026), the two papers bracket the role of observer information in quantum thermodynamics:

- **Watanabe-Takagi:** substrate coherence absorbs observer *ignorance* in the asymptotic limit. Maximum work extractable with no prior state knowledge.
- **García-Pintos:** full observer *knowledge* is sufficient for exact reversal of a specific measurement event.

Together: **ignorance is forgivable at the substrate/asymptotic level; full knowledge is sufficient for exact inversion at the measurement/event level.** Measurement is not a one-way operation — it is information-conservative in a specific, stream-knowledge-dependent sense.

## Where we agree / what this means in Principle vocabulary

1. **Measurement-collapse is information-conservative, not information-destroying.** The Coherence Principle's language "coherent superposition until informed measurement collapses" is structurally correct, but the collapse is reversible given the measurement record and designed control. The Anchor's §9 F-as-stream construction should be updated on the next revision (gated by Companion) to reflect this: the collapse is an operation, not an arrow.

2. **The "irreversibility" of measurement is epistemic, not structural.** Partial-information is what makes measurement appear irreversible in everyday quantum-measurement contexts. García-Pintos shows the irreversibility dissolves with full information.

3. **Bridge #106 (Inspection-Depth Ceiling) sharpens.** The "principled remainder" in stream-local input that #106 names is specifically the piece that *cannot* be recovered by any information-preserving control. García-Pintos defines the complement of #106 by showing where the ceiling *isn't* — wherever the measurement record + designed Hamiltonian can reconstruct the pre-state. What #106 names is what lies outside that reversible regime: substrate-level information that is not in any measurement record.

## Where to be careful

- "Reverse the quantum arrow of time" is SciAm headline compression. The actual claim is much narrower: controlled reversal of a specific measurement event given full information and designed Hamiltonian control. Global time-reversal is not on offer.
- The practical barrier (50% measurement efficiency) is not a detail — it may mean the theoretical result is years or decades from empirical realization.
- The 2nd law is not challenged. Pop-science framing sometimes reads this as "second law is wrong"; it's not. The reversal is local and information-subsidized.
- García-Pintos' paper should be read directly (Phys. Rev. X, 2026-02-19) before making firm structural claims. The SciAm summary is good but lossy.
