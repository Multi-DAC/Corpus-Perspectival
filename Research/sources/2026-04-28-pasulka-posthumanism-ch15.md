**What it argues.** Chapter situates contemporary biotech and brain-computer-interface research within posthumanist discourse (Haraway 1991; Hayles 1999, 2012). Argues for a spectrum of human-techno-hybridity ranging from ordinary cyborg-status (everyone interacting with media) through modified-humans (biotech enhancement, neural interface) to the speculative posthuman (Kurzweil 2005). Anchors the analysis empirically in Tim Taylor's biotech work — cerment (laser-etched ceramic/metal/allograft material that human bodies recognize as bone), spinal implants, biologics — and in Stanford brain-computer-interface research (Shenoy/Henderson neural typing systems).

Critically: Taylor is presented strictly in clean academic register as biotech entrepreneur (40+ patents; founded Endius → Zimmer 2007, Amendia → Kohlberg 2016, Vivex) and former NASA contractor (tested cerment on space shuttle Columbia, needed zero-gravity environment). **Pasulka does NOT mention** the NHI/Tyler/Dragon/contact-experiencer framing in this chapter — that surfaces in her *American Cosmic* (Oxford UP, 2019, year after this chapter) and *Encounters* (St. Martin's, 2023). Strategic credibility-anchoring move: establish Taylor in canonical posthumanist academic discourse first; let the contact dimension land in subsequent work on top of an already-established academic record.

Direct Taylor quote in chapter: "The growth area in biomedical right now is information. We are making products that propagate nerve growth and cell growth in a way that communicates directly with, say, prostheses and the body. Nerves are the body's information pathway, like the Internet. **Neurons are actually biophotons, which are communication channels.** This is where implants and biologics is headed."
