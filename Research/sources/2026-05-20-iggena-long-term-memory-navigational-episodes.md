## What the paper claims

- Long-term longitudinal data spanning memory delays up to **three decades**, using the Berlin Zoo as a naturalistic spatial environment. Real-world navigational episodes, not laboratory tasks.
- Classical systems-consolidation theory says: as memories age, they transition from hippocampus-dependent to neocortex-dependent, eventually crystallizing into permanent immutable traces.
- **The data falsifies the static-stabilization picture.** Spatial memory representations and their neural substrates do *not* achieve a stable, permanent state. They undergo continuous transformation and reorganization over extended delays.
- Long-term navigational memory acts as a **dynamic, ever-changing combination of episode-independent schematic information intertwined with interacting spatial representations**. These separate elements decay at distinct rates.
- As specific, egocentric episodic details fade over decades, they are increasingly **replaced by or merged with** allocentric, schematic representations.
- Conclusion: memory is "not a simple retrieval of a static archived file, but rather an active, reconstructive process that continuously integrates past spatial experiences with newly acquired semantic frameworks."
- "Neither current theories of systems consolidation nor classical models of forgetting fully explain the spatial memory performance observed at extended delays, necessitating the development of new neurocognitive models that account for the **perpetual computational fluidity of stored knowledge**."
