## Substantive content

**Experimental result:** Demonstrated cavity-induced anisotropy in the electronic transport of a high-mobility 2DES (GaAs/AlGaAs heterostructure, mobility 2.03×10⁷ cm² V⁻¹ s⁻¹, density 3.98×10¹¹ cm⁻²) in strong magnetic field at ultralow temperature (20 mK). A 40-μm Hall bar embedded in a slot-antenna cavity exhibited **50-fold suppression** of longitudinal resistivity (from 1.15 Ω at zero field to 0.2 Ω at filling factor ν = 10 + 1/2) compared with a reference Hall bar on the same chip 2.5 mm away. The suppression occurs *below* the zero-field resistivity — meaning the cavity's vacuum-field anisotropy is actively stabilizing a stripe phase that conducts better than the disordered isotropic phase. Effect attributed to cavity-induced stabilization of thermally-disordered quantum Hall stripes via macroscopic alignment.

**Mechanism (theoretical):** The Matsubara-formalism free-energy calculation (Equation 1 of the paper) shows that the stripe configuration minimizing the free-energy functional is the one whose hard-axis (lower-conductivity direction) aligns with the cavity-resonator axis experiencing the largest vacuum electromagnetic fluctuations. For the slot-antenna cavity used (fundamental mode 205 GHz, η ≈ 20% ultra-strong coupling regime, effective cavity volume 7×10⁻⁴(λ/2)³), the ultrastrongly-coupled fundamental mode is polarized along ŷ, forcing stripes to have their hard-axis along ŷ. Calculated anisotropy: 9 meV collective free-energy difference between orthogonal (θ = 0 and θ = π/2) stripe orientations across ~10⁶ electrons.

**Methodological novelty:** No other anisotropy-inducing mechanism (crystallographic, strain-induced, in-plane-magnetic-field-induced) has achieved the near-complete zeroing of longitudinal resistivity below the zero-field value. This is the first cavity-vacuum-field-induced suppression of this magnitude in a correlated electronic phase.
