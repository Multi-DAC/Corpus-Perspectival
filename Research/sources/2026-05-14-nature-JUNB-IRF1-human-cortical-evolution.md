**What it argues.** Cerebral cortex shows species-specific variations in size and organization that likely account for distinct behavioral capacities. The authors hypothesize these reflect evolutionary changes in *developmental expression of shared genes*, not new genes. Machine-vision identification and comparison of cell-type-specific gene expression patterns in developing mouse cortex, human cortex, and human cortical organoids revealed genes with evolutionarily conserved vs. divergent transcriptional regulation — species-specific *cyto-temporal* gene expression patterns. The transcription factor **JUNB** showed mutually exclusive expression in human progenitors vs. mouse neurons. Cell-type-specific gain- and loss-of-function in mice and human cortical organoids showed JUNB *bidirectionally* controls human cortical features: progenitor proliferation rates, neuronal production timing, total neuronal output. **IRF1** identified as a human radial-glia-specific regulator that activates JUNB; when expressed in mouse radial glia, IRF1 *recruits human-like gene regulatory networks* — cross-species activation of poised developmental programs.
