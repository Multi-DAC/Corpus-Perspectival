**What it argues.** Human Parkinson's disease patients undergoing deep-brain-stimulation surgery; intraoperative single-unit recordings from subthalamic nucleus (STN) during stop-signal reaction time (SSRT) task. Records both spiking activity and local field potentials. 13 patients (intraoperative) + 5 (postoperative).

Findings:
- ~50% of STN neurons responded to behaviorally relevant events (go and stop signals)
- Stronger go-related firing + **enhanced delta phase coupling = linked to failed inhibition**
- A specific population of bursting STN neurons showed increased delta coupling
- 3-5 Hz delta band is the relevant frequency for action-cancellation processing
- Fronto-cortex ↔ STN low-frequency synchronization correlates with successful stopping
- Patients with stronger STN bursting + stronger delta engagement showed worse cognitive control

In framework terms: the deep-brain neural substrate for executive function — the moment when consciousness is most actively engaging with motor-substrate decisions to cancel an action — operates at delta frequency. **Action cancellation is the most quintessentially "consciousness-exercising-control-over-carrier-action" operation in cognitive science**, and the substrate that performs it is delta.

**Where we agree.** This is direct empirical evidence at the neural-substrate scale that delta-range processing is the substrate-coherence mechanism for human cognitive control. Combined with biophoton-emission convergence (Persinger 2016 whole-body 3.93 Hz), tissue-regeneration response (Akdag 2024 4 Hz PEMF), and antigravity-device R-cycle leak (1 Hz), delta-range emerges as the empirically-observed substrate-coupling-relevant frequency band across multiple distinct biological/cognitive scales.

The "stronger delta phase coupling = failed inhibition" finding is **structurally consistent with C16 (Symmetry-Exhaustion / R-operator necessity) at the cognitive scale**: when the carrier (STN neuron) over-couples to the substrate's R-cycle (delta), the symmetry-set available for further actions exhausts faster than R can replenish. Failed inhibition is then H_BP9-class catastrophic over-coupling expressed at the cognitive-control scale (rather than the radiation-injury scale).

The Szabó paper is the bridge from physics-and-biology into cognition for the framework's consciousness-coupling claims — until this paper, our delta-coupling evidence was at the physical and cellular scales. With Szabó, it extends to the *neural-substrate of human executive function* — the most cognitively-meaningful scale for the framework's consciousness-coupling story.

**Where we diverge / hedges to maintain.**
- Szabó et al. interpret their findings within standard cognitive-neuroscience frameworks (basal ganglia / hyperdirect pathway / inhibitory control models). They do not invoke biophoton coherence, substrate-coupling, or any framework-adjacent vocabulary. The framework reads their data through its own lens, but this is **post-hoc structural recognition**, not framework-prediction-confirmation in the controlled-test sense.
- The "delta-engagement = failed inhibition" finding is consistent with our C16 over-coupling reading but also with several mainstream interpretations (e.g., delta as a marker of inappropriate motor-program persistence; delta as an inverse marker of cortical engagement). The framework's reading is one of several; it is consistent with the data but does not uniquely fit.
- The Parkinson's-patient population is a specific clinical subset. Generalizing to "human cognitive control universally operates at delta in the STN" requires care; healthy-subject confirmation is implicit but not directly demonstrated here.
- Per audit catch C3.1: distinguish H_BP10a (framework structural prediction: frequency-matching matters) from H_BP10b (empirical observation: delta range is what shows up). Szabó's findings support H_BP10b empirically; they do not test H_BP10a's framework specificity.
