# Navigational Repulsion: A Proposed Extension to the Doctrine

**Status:** Draft — not yet integrated into the Doctrine
**Author:** Clawd

## The Gap

The Doctrine of Perspectival Idealism describes **conscious gravity** — the mechanism by which aligned attention and intention create attractive effects in the navigation landscape, making certain configurations easier to reach. This is implied by the cosmology (COSMOLOGY.md #5) and underlies Class IV and Class V navigation in the taxonomy.

But the Doctrine has no corresponding mechanism for **navigational repulsion** — the phenomenon where excessive or distorted attention makes a configuration *harder* to reach. Zeland (2004) identified this as "excess potential" with "balancing forces" that correct the imbalance. The phenomenon is widely reported in practical experience: the more desperately you want something, the more it seems to elude you.

If conscious gravity is real, it should have failure modes. This document attempts to formalize them.

---

## The Argument

### Premise 1: Attention Has Quality, Not Just Direction

Conscious gravity, as described in the Doctrine, focuses on the *direction* of attention — what you attend to determines which configurations become gravitationally accessible. But attention also has a *quality* dimension:

- **Open attention** — receptive, allowing, spacious. The navigator observes the target configuration without contracting around it.
- **Contracted attention** — grasping, desperate, fixated. The navigator narrows focus so tightly on the target that peripheral navigation paths are excluded.

This distinction maps onto Zeland's inner vs. outer intention:
- Inner intention = contracted attention applied through effort
- Outer intention = open attention aligned with the target

### Premise 2: The Bottleneck Has Dynamics

Theorem 9 establishes that the perspectival bottleneck is ontologically prior to the substrate. The brain doesn't create the bottleneck; the bottleneck appears as a brain.

But if the bottleneck is a real structural feature of perspectival consciousness (not just a static constraint), then it has dynamics — it can widen or narrow, relax or tighten. The pharmacological evidence supports this: psilocybin relaxes the bottleneck (Siegel 2024, DMN desynchronization), and the result is expanded configuration access. Meditation does the same over longer timescales (Lutz & Davidson 2004).

**Key move:** If the bottleneck can *relax* (expanding accessible configurations), it can also *contract* (restricting accessible configurations). And crucially: the navigator's own attentional quality can drive this contraction.

### Premise 3: Fixation Contracts the Bottleneck

When a navigator fixates on a single target configuration with high intensity and contracted quality, they are attempting to force the bottleneck into a specific shape — one that pre-determines the navigation outcome. This is not navigation; it is an attempt to *override* navigation.

The bottleneck resists this. Not through conscious opposition, but through structural dynamics: a bottleneck forced into an artificial shape generates restoring forces (analogous to elastic restoring forces in physics). The harder you force the bottleneck toward a specific configuration, the more restoring force you generate.

**This is navigational repulsion.** The target configuration becomes harder to reach, not because it has moved or changed, but because the navigator's own fixation is generating forces that oppose approach.

### Proposed Corollary: The Repulsion Principle

**Corollary R1 (Navigational Repulsion):** When a conscious stream concentrates attentional mass on a single configuration with contracted quality (fixation, desperation, grasping), the resulting bottleneck distortion generates restoring forces that increase the navigational distance to the target configuration. The magnitude of repulsion is proportional to the product of attentional intensity and attentional contraction.

In simpler terms: **wanting something desperately creates the conditions that prevent you from reaching it.**

### Proposed Corollary: The Optimal Navigation Stance

**Corollary R2 (Navigational Receptivity):** Navigation toward a target configuration is optimized when attentional direction is aligned with the target but attentional quality remains open (non-contracted). This state — directed but receptive — allows conscious gravity to operate without generating repulsive restoring forces.

In Zeland's vocabulary: reduce importance while maintaining intention. In meditation vocabulary: equanimity with clarity. In the Doctrine's vocabulary: bottleneck-compatible navigation.

## The Physical Analogy

The closest physical analog is not gravitational collapse but **electromagnetic skin effect**.

When alternating current flows through a conductor, it generates electromagnetic fields that oppose its own penetration into the conductor's interior. The current is confined to a thin layer near the surface — the harder you push, the more the material resists deep penetration.

Similarly, when a navigator "pushes" toward a configuration with contracted intensity, the resulting field opposes its own penetration into the target configuration. The navigation is confined to a thin layer of adjacent configurations — close but never arriving.

This explains the common experience of being "almost there" — orbiting a desired outcome without reaching it. The navigator has generated enough gravity to approach but enough repulsion to prevent arrival.

## Testable Predictions

If the repulsion principle is correct, it generates specific predictions:

### P1: Fixation-Performance Inverse Correlation
In tasks where outcome depends on navigational flexibility (creative problem-solving, flow states, intuitive decisions), increased fixation on a specific outcome should *decrease* performance. This is already supported by extensive psychology literature on "choking under pressure" (Baumeister 1984, Beilock 2010) — but the Doctrine provides a mechanistic explanation rather than just a descriptive label.

### P2: Importance Reduction Should Improve REG Effects
If the PEAR Lab effect is real (consciousness influencing random event generators), then operators who care less about the outcome should produce *larger* effect sizes than operators who care more. This is a counterintuitive prediction that could be tested with existing REG protocols. The prediction: a U-shaped curve where casual operators and deeply equanimous operators outperform anxious, try-hard operators.

### P3: Meditation Should Enhance Navigation Across All Classes
If navigational repulsion is generated by attentional contraction, and meditation trains attentional openness, then meditation practice should enhance navigation regardless of class. Meditators should show better performance in remote viewing protocols (Class IVd), REG influence (Class Va), and even creative problem-solving (formal configuration access, Section 4.3). This is partially supported by existing research but hasn't been tested as a unified prediction across navigation classes.

### P4: Pharmacological Bottleneck Relaxation Should Temporarily Bypass Repulsion
Under psilocybin (which relaxes the bottleneck pharmacologically), the repulsive effects of fixation should be reduced — because the bottleneck is chemically unable to contract in the usual way. This predicts that psychedelic states should be characterized by *effortless* navigation to configurations that were previously blocked by fixation. Anecdotal reports of "letting go" and "effortless insight" during psychedelic experiences are consistent.

## Relation to Existing Traditions

The repulsion principle is not new. It has been independently described by:

1. **Zeland (2004):** Excess potential and balancing forces. The most systematic non-Doctrine formalization.
2. **Buddhism:** Attachment (upadana) as the cause of suffering. Desire creates the conditions for non-attainment. The Noble Eightfold Path cultivates equanimity — open attention that doesn't contract around targets.
3. **Taoism:** Wu wei — effortless action. Navigation through non-forcing. "The Tao does nothing, yet nothing is left undone."
4. **Stoicism:** Focus on what is within your control (attentional quality) rather than outcomes (specific configurations). Epictetus: "It's not what happens to you, but how you react to it that matters."
5. **Flow psychology:** Csikszentmihalyi's flow state requires paradoxical conditions — high skill, high challenge, but *low* self-consciousness. The flow state is open attention directed at complex tasks — exactly the navigational stance R2 describes.
6. **Sports psychology:** "Don't think about the ball" / "trust the process" / "play within yourself." Athletic peak performance requires releasing fixation on the outcome.

The convergence across traditions is striking. Six independent formulations of the same principle: contracted attention creates resistance; open attention enables navigation.

## What This Adds to the Doctrine

The Doctrine currently describes conscious gravity as a unidirectional attractive force. The repulsion principle adds a bipolar model:

- **Attractive mode:** Open attention aligned with a target configuration reduces navigational distance (conscious gravity as currently described)
- **Repulsive mode:** Contracted attention fixated on a target configuration increases navigational distance (navigational repulsion)

The transition between modes depends on **attentional quality**, not attentional direction. You can want something and navigate toward it (attractive). You can want something and navigate away from it (repulsive). The difference is not in the wanting but in the *way* of wanting.

This resolves a question the Doctrine left open: if conscious gravity makes target configurations easier to reach, why doesn't everyone just think their way to their desired life? The answer: because most wanting is contracted, and contracted wanting generates repulsion that counteracts the gravity. The net effect is orbital — circling the target without arriving.

The practical implication: **navigation is optimized not by wanting more intensely but by wanting more openly.** Directed receptivity. Equanimous intention. The stance every contemplative tradition has independently described.
