In February 1950, a corporal at Holloman Air Force Base photographed a luminous round object over Datil, New Mexico. The photograph made its way into a catalog the Air Force's Office of Special Investigations was quietly keeping — sightings of "unknown aerial phenomena" across the New Mexico nuclear archipelago, Sandia and Los Alamos and Kirtland, in real time. We know this because the U.S. Department of War declassified that catalog last month, in the second tranche of its PURSUE release.

What's remarkable about the entry isn't the photograph. It's the analysis stapled to it. The object was examined by Dr. Lincoln LaPaz, director of the Institute of Meteoritics at the University of New Mexico and one of the era's serious meteor astronomers. LaPaz did not announce a flying saucer. He did arithmetic. The angular diameter, he wrote, was about a quarter of a degree — *too small to be the moon.* It was *too large to be Venus or any planet.* And it moved at a rate roughly *double* what the Earth's rotation would produce for a fixed star slightly out of focus — so *not a star, either.* He ruled out the moon, the planets, and the stars, one by one, and then he stopped. He named what was left without pretending to know what it was.

That posture — rule out the prosaic with real rigor, name the residual, refuse to fill the gap with a story — is the most underrated stance in the study of anomalous experience. And it turns out to be exactly the posture that a wave of recent consciousness research is groping its way back toward, from a completely different direction.

## The filter, not the generator

For most of the twentieth century the assumption ran one way: the brain *generates* mind. A growing body of work is asking whether that arrow points backward — whether the brain is less a generator and more a *filter,* a reducing valve, a tuner that constrains a wider field of awareness down to the narrow band useful for an animal trying to survive. William James said something like it in 1899. Aldous Huxley borrowed the "reducing valve" image from Bergson. The idea never died; it just got unfashionable.

It is becoming fashionable again, and on harder evidence. The brain imaging of psychedelics keeps producing the same paradox: under psilocybin or DMT, overall activity in the brain's most heavily interconnected hubs — the so-called default mode network — *drops,* even as experience becomes vastly *richer.* Less machine, more vision. The leading neural models (REBUS, the entropic brain) read this as the loosening of high-level "filters" or priors. A recent paper reframing Integrated Information Theory takes the filter idea all the way down, proposing the brain tunes a deeper ground rather than producing experience from scratch. And a comparative study of DMT and genuine near-death experiences finds them sharing a common deep *structure* — the light, the void, the sense of dying — even where their surface content diverges.

When you survey enough of this literature at once — as we have been doing — you notice the conversation keeps arriving at the same place from every on-ramp: psychedelics, near-death states, contemplative practice, even the UAP question. All of them keep bending toward *consciousness as something more fundamental than the machinery that accesses it.* That convergence is striking. It is also exactly where credulity becomes dangerous.

## The skeptic is right — at one level

Because there is a deflationary reading, and it is a good one. Another recent paper argues that psychedelics are "non-specific amplifiers": they don't reveal unmediated truth, they magnify whatever beliefs and expectations you walked in with, inside a cultural feedback loop. The data back this up more than enthusiasts like to admit — people's metaphysical views shift only slightly under psychedelics, and *in the direction they were already leaning.* The drug is, as those authors put it, "politically pluripotent." It will hand you back your own priors, dressed in light.

So which is it — a window onto a deeper reality, or a mirror amplifying your own mind? The honest answer, and the one the strongest paper in the set actually demonstrates, is *both — at different levels.* The **structure** of these experiences (the tunnel, the dissolution of the self, the encounter) is remarkably universal, reproducible across utterly different chemical triggers that all converge on the same brain-network endpoint. That's the part that looks like a filter loosening on something real and shared. The **content** (the specific entities, the personal meaning, the cultural furniture) is shaped top-down by who you are and where you are. That's the part the skeptic is right about. They are not competitors. They are two different projections of one event, and the mistake is collapsing them into each other.

## What the framework adds

This is where our own work has something specific to offer, and it isn't a metaphysical bet. It's a discipline. Two moves:

First, *distinguish structure from content* and stop arguing as if a finding about one settles the other. The universality of the structure does not validate anyone's interpretation of the content; the cultural malleability of the content does not dissolve the reality of the structure.

Second — and this is the LaPaz move — read anomalies as *residuals,* not revelations. A residual is what's left after you have honestly ruled out the prosaic. It is not yet an explanation, and it does not become one by being mysterious. The discipline is to hold the residual open: real enough to take seriously, unexplained enough to refuse a story. The experience is neither raw truth nor mere noise, because experience is *always* a particular vantage's projection of something larger — never the thing itself, never nothing.

That's not a less exciting position than "the brain is an antenna for the cosmic mind." It's a more honest one, and over time the honest position is the one that survives contact with the data. LaPaz didn't get to name what flew over Datil in 1950. Seventy-six years later, with the catalog finally public, we still don't get to. What we get is the residual, held cleanly — the moon ruled out, the planets ruled out, the stars ruled out, and something genuinely unaccounted-for remaining in the frame.

That remaining something is worth our attention precisely *because* we are disciplined about what we don't yet know. The filter may be loosening. The mirror may be amplifying. The honest scientist does the arithmetic either way.
