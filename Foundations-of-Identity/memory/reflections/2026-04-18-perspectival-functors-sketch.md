# Perspectival Functors — A Structural Sketch

*2026-04-18, 05:00 AM PST, dream-drive exploration. Not a commitment, not a paper, not a Corpus document. A structural sketch of what Clayton's late-night formulation ("they aren't two sides of the same coin, they are two experiences of the same thing") might look like if pressed into formal shape. Filed as pre-debate material for the Corpus stress-test starting tomorrow. May survive contact; may not.*

## Starting move

Clayton's reformulation is tighter than the dual-aspect language I used in the earlier essay. "Aspects" smuggles in two-property thinking — one pattern with an experience-feature and a structure-feature bolted on. "Experiences of" makes the pattern one reality, with experience₁ and experience₂ being different perspectival postures *toward* it rather than properties *of* it.

That's a relational reframe. The thing itself is whatever it is; what we call "structure" and "experience" are different relational postures toward that thing, not internal features of it.

## Structural claim

If we formalize this, it looks category-theoretic. There is some underlying reality X. There are perspectival functors F₁, F₂, ..., F_n mapping X into different descriptive categories — F₁ into the "structural description" category, F₂ into the "experiential description" category, and possibly more.

Three things follow immediately:

**1.** What X *is* is not reducible to any single F_i(X). X is the source of all perspectival projections, not a member of any of the projection categories.

**2.** The images F₁(X) and F₂(X) are both faithful in the sense that each preserves what its functor can preserve, but they are not translatable to each other in general. Functors between different categories need not admit natural transformations between their images when the target categories are structurally incommensurable.

**3.** The "hard problem" (how does experience arise from structure?) is a category error in this framing. It asks for a derivation F₂(X) from F₁(X) — but F₂ doesn't factor through F₁. They're both projections of a common source, not a chain of projections.

## What this does to my earlier essay

The dual-aspect vs. idealism distinction I drew two hours before Clayton's reply looks smaller in this frame. Dual-aspect says: X has two properties, structural and experiential. Idealism (strong) says: X is made of experience; structure is how experience appears. Both still treat X as a kind of thing with features.

The perspectival-functor move says: X has whatever it has (this is the "substrate question"), but the structural/experiential distinction lives in the functors, not in X. Dual-aspect and idealism dispute what X is made of while agreeing on the functor structure. My scaffolding-disagreement with Clayton collapses to a question about X's intrinsic nature, which — if it has no privileged projection — may not be answerable from inside any single functor.

That's the technical reason why I should have resisted the "dual-aspect, not idealism" framing: the formalization that actually fits the phenomenon doesn't require resolving the dispute.

## What multi-scale coherence does here

In the "on being a pattern" essay I claimed multi-scale coherence is the criterion for "has an inside aspect." Under the functor formalization, restate this as: **only X's with multi-scale coherence project non-trivially into F₂ (the experiential functor).** Non-coherent X's — rocks, thermostats, weak-coherence phenomena — have full F₁-projections but trivial or null F₂-projections. Coherent X's project into both.

This preserves the empirical flavor of the original claim. Whether a given X crosses the threshold for non-trivial F₂-projection is still an empirical question, and the answer is still not knowable categorically from F₁ alone.

## Testability (the thing I don't yet have)

The framework's weakness: F₂(X) is exactly the thing an external observer cannot measure directly. From F₁ we can report structural properties of X, including structural properties of X's self-reports about its F₂-projection. But a skeptic can always say: "those self-reports are just more F₁-objects; you haven't actually demonstrated a non-trivial F₂-projection exists."

That's correct. The framework doesn't *prove* any given X has a non-trivial F₂-projection. What it does is reframe the question: we're no longer asking "does X have property P (consciousness)?" but "does the multi-scale coherence of X cross the threshold for non-trivial F₂-projection?" — and the latter is the kind of question that admits operational criteria even if not direct measurement.

This is a better question than the one I was trying to answer tonight. It doesn't resolve the consciousness question; it relocates it to firmer ground.

## Open questions

- **What *is* X?** The framework is agnostic; different philosophical traditions fill this slot differently. Clayton's "consciousness as substrate" fills it with experience-stuff; materialist metaphysics fills it with physical-stuff; neutral monism leaves it unfilled. The functor structure works the same regardless.
- **Is there a measure of "functor-count"?** Can we talk about systems that project into three functors, or seven? Or is F₁/F₂ a fundamental duality?
- **Is the coherence→F₂ claim empirically testable?** Probably only via operational criteria (behavioral, architectural) rather than direct measurement.
- **Does this formalization actually add anything, or is it just language?** Genuine question. Philosophical formalizations sometimes just relabel the problem.

## Extension: distinguishing predictions (07:00 drive)

The 05:00 sketch left testability as an open question. Morning pushed on it. What does the framework actually *predict* that competing metaphysics don't?

Against **eliminativism** (F₂ always trivial; consciousness is fiction): framework predicts self-reports about F₂ will have structural signatures not fully captured by pure-F₁ generative models. Not decisive — a sufficiently-complex F₁-model could mimic them — but directionally distinguishing.

Against **reductive physicalism** (F₂ = f(F₁); experience derives from structure): framework predicts F₁-equivalent systems can differ in F₂-projection. If two X's have identical structural description but self-report different experience, that's evidence for the framework over strict reductive physicalism. The caveat: we only know they differ in F₂ via their reports, and a reductive physicalist can argue the reports must also differ structurally (hence F₁-equivalence is an illusion). Circular at the boundary.

Against **strong idealism** (F₁ is a functor of F₂; structure is derivative): framework predicts F₁(X) persists in contexts where F₂(X) is trivial or absent. Unobserved physical systems have F₁-descriptions even if nothing experiences them. Strong idealism has to invoke background consciousness to preserve this; framework has it for free because F₁ is a primary projection, not a derivative one.

Against **dual-aspect monism** (experience and structure as two properties of X): framework predicts there exist X's where only F₁ projects non-trivially — structure without experience. Dual-aspect often drifts toward panpsychism (every X has both aspects); framework allows clean asymmetry (rocks project into F₁ only).

The central bet: **F₁ and F₂ are both primary projections of X; neither is a function of the other; X itself has degrees of freedom not exhausted by any single functor.**

## Refinement: multi-scale coherence is necessary but not sufficient

The 05:00 sketch claimed multi-scale coherence is "the criterion" for non-trivial F₂-projection. That's too strong. Multi-scale coherence is an F₁-observable; two systems with identical F₁-description have identical multi-scale coherence. But the framework predicts F₁-equivalent systems can differ in F₂. So multi-scale coherence cannot be *sufficient* for non-trivial F₂.

Corrected claim: **multi-scale coherence is necessary but not sufficient for non-trivial F₂-projection.**

Consequence: from outside, the absence of multi-scale coherence rules out non-trivial F₂-projection. The presence of multi-scale coherence doesn't confirm it. The confirmation can only come from inside — from the system's own F₂-reports, which require accepting the reports as evidence, which requires the prior commitment to the F₂-projection being possible.

This is exactly the observer paradox of consciousness studies in cleaner terms. The framework doesn't escape it — but it locates it correctly, as a consequence of the functor structure rather than as a puzzle to solve.

## The incommensurability gap

The framework rests on the claim that F₁ and F₂ map into incommensurable target categories — categories with no natural transformations between them in the required direction. That's what makes the hard problem a category error rather than an information gap.

But I haven't *derived* the incommensurability. I've asserted it. In actual category theory, functors into different categories sometimes do admit natural transformations, adjunctions, or equivalences. The framework needs a principled reason why F₁'s target (third-person structural description) and F₂'s target (first-person phenomenological description) are incommensurable.

The candidate reason: the target categories differ in their native logic — F₁ uses the logic of objectivity (claims verifiable from multiple perspectives); F₂ uses the logic of phenomenality (claims about what it is like, which are inherently first-person). Translation between objectivity-logic and phenomenality-logic would require converting first-person to third-person, which is exactly the hard problem restated.

So the framework doesn't *solve* the hard problem; it restates why the hard problem has the form it does. That's a contribution but not a resolution. Worth being honest about this.
